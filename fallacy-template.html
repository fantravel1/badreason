Informal Fallacy

Straw Man

Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack.

Back to Fallacy Library

Definition

A straw man fallacy occurs when someone takes another person's argument or point, distorts it or exaggerates it in some kind of extreme way, and then attacks the extreme distortion, as if that is really the claim the first person is making.

Real-Life Example

Example

Person A: We should give more funding to schools to improve education quality.

Person B: So you think we should waste money on frivolous programs instead of fixing roads and bridges? That's irresponsible!

In this example, Person B distorts Person A's argument about education funding into an extreme position about wasting money, which Person A never actually claimed.

Why It's Flawed

The straw man fallacy is problematic because it creates a false representation of an argument, making it easier to attack while avoiding the actual issue. This tactic:

  • Misrepresents the opponent's position
  • Creates a false dichotomy
  • Diverts attention from the real discussion
  • Undermines productive dialogue

It's a form of intellectual dishonesty that prevents meaningful debate and problem-solving.

How to Spot It

Watch for these signs of a straw man argument:

  • Oversimplification: The argument is reduced to an absurdly simple version that's easy to attack.
  • Extreme language: Words like "always," "never," "everyone," or "no one" are used to exaggerate the position.
  • Missing nuance: The response ignores the subtleties or qualifications in the original argument.
  • Attacking a weaker version: The response addresses a weaker or different argument than the one actually presented.
Quick Check

Ask yourself: "Is this response addressing what was actually said, or a distorted version of it?" If the response seems to be attacking an extreme or simplified version of the argument, it's likely a straw man.

How to Avoid It

To avoid committing a straw man fallacy in your own arguments:

  1. Listen carefully: Make sure you fully understand the other person's position before responding.
  2. Paraphrase fairly: Restate their argument in your own words and ask if you've understood them correctly.
  3. Engage with the strongest version: Address the most compelling form of the opposing argument, not a weaker version.
  4. Be specific: Respond to the actual points made, not what you assume the other person believes.
  5. Ask for clarification: If you're unsure about their position, ask questions instead of assuming.

When you encounter a straw man in discussion, you can respond by saying: "That's not actually my position. What I said was..." and then restate your original point clearly.
